This resource addresses civil law only. It does not challenge Catholic doctrine on sacramental marriage. It focuses on the state's duty to protect legal rights, children, and personal dignity when a marriage is irreparably broken.

I. Why This Matters in Practice

The absence of absolute divorce does not prevent marital breakdown. It changes who is allowed to recover from it. In practice, the current framework:

  • Traps women in abusive or permanently abandoned marriages.
  • Disproportionately harms the poor by making legal relief effectively paywalled.
  • Forces legal gray zones that undermine respect for law (void foreign marriages, bigamy risk, unstable custody/support arrangements).
  • Creates dependence on foreigners by making legal capacity to remarry hinge on a foreign spouse's divorce and subsequent recognition.

III. Catholic Teaching and the State's Limited Role

Catholic doctrine upholds the indissolubility of sacramental marriage. The civil state, however, regulates civil effects: property, custody, support, legal capacity, and public order.

The Catechism explicitly recognizes that civil divorce can be tolerated when it is the only possible way to ensure legal rights, care of children, or protection of inheritance (CCC §2383).

Catechism reference (primary)

CCC §2383: If civil divorce remains the only possible way of ensuring certain legal rights, the care of the children, or the protection of inheritance, it can be tolerated and does not constitute a moral offense.

Source: Vatican text (CCC 2383)

What legislators are deciding

Not whether the sacrament changes — it does not. The question is whether the State continues enforcing civil bondage in situations where protection of dignity, children, and legal rights require a civil remedy.
